Set 08

Hard to Name

You often feel more than one thing at once. Pleased and resentful. Relieved and bereft. Grateful and, somehow, also sad. Most of the time there is pressure — from yourself or from the people around you — to pick one and go with it. So you do, and something real gets quietly set aside.

These cards work in the gap between what you feel and what you call it. Some ask you to stay with a feeling before you name it. Some ask you to move when the feeling has not resolved yet. None of them require you to arrive at an answer.

You won't come out of these with tidier emotions. But you might get better at noticing the full shape of what is actually there — which turns out to be more useful than a clean label.

Drawn from Anthony G. Vaccaro, “Feelings are Messy: The Feelings We Study in Affective Science Should Be Too,” Affective Science, 2024. doi.org/10.1007/s42761-024-00263-z

M · Monitor without a label

An encounter with what you feel before you name it.

⏱ 10 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Think of a moment from the last 24 hours when you felt something but couldn't quite describe it, or when you named it quickly and moved on. Write down what happened.
  2. 2.Sit with the feeling for two minutes without reaching for a word. Notice whether there's more than one thing happening, or whether the feeling shifted as the moment unfolded.
  3. 3.Write what you can: not a label, but what it was actually like. Where you felt it. Whether it stayed steady or changed.
Notice: whether the feeling became clearer or more complicated when you gave it more room.

No outcome required.

E · Execute before it settles

An encounter with acting when you don't yet know how you feel.

⏱ 10 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Think of something you've been putting off. Not because it's too hard, but because you're not sure how you feel about it yet.
  2. 2.Do one small piece of it. Five minutes of work is enough. While you're moving, notice what the feeling does.
  3. 3.When you stop, write one sentence. Not about how you feel now, but about what the feeling did while you were working. Did it shift? Sharpen? Disappear?
Notice: whether moving changed the feeling, or just revealed it more clearly.

No outcome required.

S · Support let it be mixed

An encounter with someone else's complicated feeling.

⏱ 15 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Think of someone carrying something complicated right now. Not clearly happy or sad, or changing from one day to the next. Write down the single word you'd normally use to describe what they're going through.
  2. 2.Now write three to four sentences about what doesn't fit that word. The parts that are harder to see, or that seem to contradict the simple version.
  3. 3.The next time you're with them, try asking about one of those parts. Not to fix it, just to acknowledge it's there.
Notice: whether they seem surprised that you noticed the harder-to-name part.

No outcome required.

S · Surrender not knowing

An encounter with a feeling you can't identify.

⏱ 10 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Find a feeling you currently have that you can't name. Something present but unclear. If nothing surfaces immediately, sit quietly for two minutes. Something will.
  2. 2.Stay with it without trying to resolve it. Set a timer for five minutes. When you want to label it, notice the impulse. But don't follow it yet.
  3. 3.When the timer ends, write one phrase that gets close. Not a single word, but a phrase. "Something like dread but also curiosity" counts. Accuracy matters less than honesty.
Notice: whether the not-knowing itself has a texture. Heavy or light, familiar or strange.

No outcome required.

Y · Yield what the combination tells you

An encounter with what two feelings together reveal that neither one alone would.

⏱ 15 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Think of a time when you felt two things at once: proud and embarrassed, relieved and sad, grateful and resentful. Pick one specific moment.
  2. 2.Write about each feeling separately: two to three sentences each. What was the proud part actually about? What was the embarrassed part about? Don't try to reconcile them.
  3. 3.Read both back. Notice whether the combination points to something that a single label would have missed entirely.
Notice: what the contradiction was protecting or pointing toward.

No outcome required.
