Set 04

Good Enough

Herbert Simon won the Nobel Prize for an idea most people resist: humans do not optimize. We satisfice. We consider a limited set of options, choose one that is good enough, and move on. The alternative — searching endlessly for the best — is not a higher standard. It is a different kind of cost.

These five encounters ask you to look at where you are still searching. Each card takes one MESSY dimension and finds the place where the pursuit of the optimal is consuming energy that belongs somewhere else.

Good enough is not a consolation. For Simon, it was a strategy.

Inspired by David Epstein, “The Nobel-Winning Psychologist Who Believed He Found the Secret to Happiness,” The New York Times, May 12, 2026 — drawing on the work of Herbert A. Simon, Models of My Life, 1991

M · Monitor how you actually decide

An encounter with your own tendencies as a decision-maker.

⏱ 10 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Think about a significant decision you made in the last year — a job, a relationship, a purchase, a path. Write down how you made it. Did you consider a few options and choose, or did you keep searching for something better?
  2. 2.Simon observed that humans cannot evaluate every available option — there are too many, our information is incomplete, and our minds aren't built to weigh them all. So we satisfice: we consider a limited set of options, choose one that is good enough, and move on. Write one honest sentence about whether you satisfice or maximize in this area of your life.
  3. 3.Name one area where you satisfice well. Name one where you can't seem to stop searching.
Notice: whether your searching is serving you — or haunting you.

No outcome required.

E · Execute the good enough move

An encounter with action that doesn't wait for certainty.

⏱ 10 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Simon lived by one mantra: "The best is the enemy of the good." He wore one brand of socks, owned one black beret, ate the same breakfast every morning, and lived in the same house for 46 years. He wasn't being eccentric. He was protecting his attention for the work that mattered. Name one recurring decision in your life that is consuming more attention than it deserves.
  2. 2.Decide it now. In one sentence. For the next 30 days.
  3. 3.Write down what that decision has been costing you — in time, energy, or mental space.
Notice: what becomes available when one small decision is already made.

No outcome required.

S · Support what your constraints are protecting

An encounter with the possibility that your limits are doing something useful.

⏱ 15 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Simon's daughter Katherine said her father "simplified his life in terms of his daily habits, thus eliminating the need to make little decisions about everything." That simplification freed his attention for the people and work that actually mattered to him. Name one real constraint in your life right now — time, energy, money, capacity.
  2. 2.Ask: what does this constraint force you to prioritize? What gets your full attention because of it, not in spite of it?
  3. 3.Sit with this question: if this constraint disappeared tomorrow, what would you risk losing focus on?
Notice: whether the constraint is only a problem — or also a structure.

No outcome required.

S · Surrender searching for the best

An encounter with the exhaustion of not being able to stop comparing.

⏱ 10 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Simon argued that searching for the best is itself a cost — one most people forget to account for. Name one area of your life where you are still searching for the optimal answer. A decision, a path, a version of yourself.
  2. 2.Write down how long you've been searching. Be honest.
  3. 3.Don't resolve it. Just sit with this: what has the search cost you so far?
Notice: the difference between genuine discernment and the performance of thoroughness.

No outcome required.

Y · Yield saving it for what matters

An encounter with the freedom that comes from deciding in advance.

⏱ 10 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Simon's core insight was simple: stop spending cognitive resources on decisions that don't deserve them. Save them for the work that does. Write down the three things that matter most to you right now — the places where your best thinking genuinely needs to go.
  2. 2.Now identify one decision you are currently maximizing (searching endlessly for the perfect answer) that has nothing to do with those three things.
  3. 3.Set a good-enough standard for it. Write it down in one sentence.
Notice: whether the energy you free up actually goes where you intended.

No outcome required.
