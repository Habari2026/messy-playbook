Set 10

The Messy Middle

Most people treat the messy middle — that uncomfortable stretch where your old understanding has stopped fitting and a new one hasn't arrived yet — as a problem to get through. So they reach for a quick explanation, force a decision before it's ready, or just wait it out and hope the confusion clears on its own. None of those moves are wrong, exactly. But they all treat the middle as a waiting room, and it isn't one.

These five cards work inside the confusion rather than around it. Some ask you to name where your old way of seeing something has stopped working. Some ask you to act before things are fully clear. One asks you to set down an explanation you've been holding on to — not permanently, just long enough to see what's actually underneath it.

You won't come out of these with the mess resolved. But you might find that the middle you've been trying to escape is actually where most of the useful thinking happens — and that staying in it a little longer turns out to be the move.

Drawn from Tina Cook, “The Purpose of Mess in Action Research: Building Rigour through a Messy Turn,” Educational Action Research, 17(2), 2009. doi.org/10.1080/09650790902914241

M · Monitor the old map

An encounter with the understanding you've been navigating by — and where it has stopped matching the territory.

⏱ 10 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Think of a situation that keeps feeling confusing or stuck. Write it down in one sentence.
  2. 2.Ask: what did you used to think about this that no longer quite fits? Write that down — the old explanation, the old story, the old assumption.
  3. 3.Notice whether you have been trying to force the old understanding back into place, or waiting for a new one to arrive fully formed.
Notice: what it feels like to name the gap without immediately trying to fill it.

No outcome required.

E · Execute move anyway

An encounter with one thing you can do from inside the confusion — before it clears.

⏱ 10 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Name something you have been waiting to act on until you understood it better. Not a practical obstacle — a feeling that you don't yet know what you actually think or want.
  2. 2.Ask: what would you do next if you accepted that the understanding might only come from moving, not from more thinking?
  3. 3.Write down one action that feels possible from where you are now — not from where you want to be.
Notice: whether waiting for clarity has become its own kind of stalling.

No outcome required.

S · Support someone to stay in it with

An encounter with the difference between someone who helps you tidy the mess up and someone who helps you work through it.

⏱ 15 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Think of something confusing or unresolved you are currently carrying. Who have you talked to about it?
  2. 2.For each person, ask honestly: did they help you understand it better, or help you feel better about it? Both matter — but they're different things.
  3. 3.Think of one person who tends to keep the conversation going rather than closing it down. What would it look like to bring this mess to them?
Notice: whether you have been seeking comfort over clarity — and whether that has been a deliberate choice.

No outcome required.

S · Surrender put it down for now

An encounter with an explanation you have been holding on to because the alternative is not knowing.

⏱ 10 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Name something you have already decided — a reason something happened, a story about why things are the way they are, a conclusion you landed on quickly.
  2. 2.Ask: what would you have to sit with if you set that explanation down for now? Not permanently — just for today.
  3. 3.Set it down. Don't replace it with another explanation. Just notice what is actually there without the frame.
Notice: whether the explanation has been more about comfort than accuracy.

No outcome required.

Y · Yield what staying in it made possible

An encounter with something that only became visible because you didn't resolve the confusion too soon.

⏱ 15 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Think of a time you stayed in genuine confusion — about a situation, a relationship, a decision — longer than was comfortable, and came out the other side seeing it differently.
  2. 2.Write down what staying in that middle actually produced. Not a general lesson — something specific that shifted.
  3. 3.Ask: what would have been lost if you had resolved it earlier?
Notice: whether the confusion and the clarity were part of the same process, not opposites.

No outcome required.
