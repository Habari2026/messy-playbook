Set 13

Optimizing Happiness

There is a particular kind of disappointment that arrives during good moments. You are somewhere pleasant, or something is going well, and somewhere in the middle of it a part of you steps back and starts taking stock. Is this as good as it should be? Am I enjoying this enough? The moment is still happening, but you are no longer quite inside it. You are rating it instead.

These five cards work with that pattern directly. Each one approaches it from a different angle — noticing when the shift happens, trying something without grading it, looking at what the habit costs you. None of them ask you to stop wanting good things. They ask you to notice what gets in the way of having them.

You may not come away from these cards feeling happier. What tends to happen instead is that you get a clearer picture of where you've been standing in your own way — and what it might look like to step aside.

Inspired by Iris B. Mauss, Maya Tamir, Craig L. Anderson, and Nicole S. Savino, “Can Seeking Happiness Make People Happy? Paradoxical Effects of Valuing Happiness,” Emotion, 2011; and Felicia K. Zerwas, Brett Q. Ford, Oliver P. John, and Iris B. Mauss, “Unpacking the Pursuit of Happiness,” Emotion, 2024.

M · Monitor the running check

An encounter with the moment you stepped out of something good.

⏱ 10 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Think of a recent experience that should have felt good — a meal, a conversation, a moment off, a small win. Something where the conditions were right. Write it down in a sentence.
  2. 2.Try to remember what was actually going through your mind during it. Not what you felt — what you were thinking. Were you rating it? Comparing it to something? Wondering if you were enjoying it enough?
  3. 3.Write down the thought that was running in the background. You don't have to judge it. Just name it.
Notice: whether the background thought showed up before the moment ended or while it was still happening.

No outcome required.

E · Execute just this

An encounter with doing one thing without rating it.

⏱ 10 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Choose something small you enjoy — or used to enjoy — that you can do right now. It doesn't have to be special. Take a short walk, listen to one song, or make something simple to eat.
  2. 2.While you're doing it, notice the moment you start evaluating — whether it's good enough, whether you're enjoying it enough, whether it's living up to what you wanted. When that thought arrives, don't follow it. Return to the physical experience: what you can see, hear, or feel right now.
  3. 3.When the time is up, write one sentence about what the experience was actually like — not whether it was good.
Notice: how many times the evaluating thought arrived, and how quickly it came.

No outcome required.

S · Support their version of fine

An encounter with someone else's good enough.

⏱ 15 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Think of someone in your life who seems easier on themselves about happiness than you are — someone who can enjoy something without seeming to grade it. It could be anyone: a friend, a family member, a colleague.
  2. 2.Write down one or two things you've noticed about how they move through good moments. Not what they do to pursue happiness — how they seem to be when it's already there.
  3. 3.Write down one thing that approach makes possible for them that your approach makes harder for you.
Notice: what the gap between their approach and yours reveals about the standard you've been holding yourself to.

No outcome required.

S · Surrender good enough to leave alone

An encounter with a moment that doesn't need a verdict.

⏱ 10 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Think of something coming up in the next day or two that you'd like to enjoy — something low-stakes and genuinely pleasant. Write it down.
  2. 2.Write down the standard you're already bringing to it. What would make it a good experience? What would make it fall short? Be honest — most of us have a picture in mind before we arrive.
  3. 3.Now write down what it would mean to show up without that standard. Not with low expectations — with no measurement at all. What would you actually be paying attention to instead?
Notice: whether releasing the standard feels like relief or like losing something.

No outcome required.

Y · Yield what was actually there

An encounter with an experience you may have partially missed.

⏱ 15 minutes

The Mess

  1. 1.Think of a time when something good was happening but part of you was watching and grading instead of being there. Write down what the situation was.
  2. 2.Set the evaluation aside for a moment. Write down what was actually present in that experience — the specific details of it, the parts that were genuinely good, the things you would have noticed more if you hadn't been assessing.
  3. 3.Write one sentence about what was there that you didn't quite let yourself have.
Notice: whether this feels like loss or like something that's still available to you.

No outcome required.
